Can an AI be your AA sponsor? An honest answer.

By [BYLINE TO BE DECIDED] • Published 22 July 2026 • Updated 22 July 2026 • 6 min read
The short answer

No. An AI cannot be your AA sponsor, and any product telling you otherwise is selling you something.

A sponsor is someone who has done the steps and takes you through them. They know your story. They notice when your voice changes. They can say the thing you do not want to hear, because they have earned the right to say it, and they can meet you for coffee at ten on a Tuesday. None of that is available to software.

What an AI can do is cover the hours in between. The hard moments rarely happen in a meeting. They happen at 3am, on a Sunday, in the twenty minutes after a phone call goes badly, when calling your sponsor for the third time this week feels like too much. That gap is real, and it is where an AI is genuinely useful.

Use it as a bridge to people, never as a replacement for them.

What does a sponsor actually do?

It is worth being specific, because the answer is the whole reason software cannot take the job.

A sponsor takes you through the steps. Not explains them, takes you through them. They have done the work themselves, which means when you get to the fourth step and start writing a version of your life where you were mostly the wronged party, they recognise it, because they wrote that same inventory once.

A sponsor also knows your history. Not a summary of it. They remember the thing you said in month two that you have since talked yourself out of. They know which of your explanations are true and which ones you use when you are about to do something.

And a sponsor has standing. They can tell you that you are lying to yourself, and it lands, because they are a person who has been where you are and who chose to pick up the phone. Nothing you can install has that standing. Being told an uncomfortable truth by software is easy to dismiss, and it should be.

Last, a sponsor is a relationship with obligations running both ways. You show up for them too. That reciprocity is doing quiet work: it puts you in a position of being needed, often for the first time in a while, and that is part of what keeps people sober.

What can an AI actually do?

Fewer things, and they are worth naming honestly.

  • It answers immediately, at any hour. No waiting, no wondering whether you are being a burden, no calculating whether 4am is too late to call.
  • It does not get tired of you. You can ask the same question for the ninth time. You can go over the same resentment again. There is no cost to you for doing it, and no expression on anyone's face.
  • It lowers the price of the first sentence. Plenty of people will type something they are not ready to say aloud. Getting it out of your head and into words is often the thing that makes it possible to say it to a person later.
  • It remembers what you told it. Not the way a person who loves you remembers, but well enough to notice when the same thing keeps coming up.
  • It can point you at the steps and the literature. It can walk through the structure of an inventory with you at midnight so that you arrive at your actual sponsor with something written.

Notice that most of these are about availability and friction, not about wisdom. That is the honest shape of it. An AI is not a better sponsor than a person. It is a worse one that happens to be awake.

So what is it actually for?

The hours in between.

Recovery is not lived in meetings. Meetings are an hour, a few times a week. The rest of it is the commute, the family dinner, the anniversary, the Sunday afternoon with nothing in it, the argument that leaves you shaking. Your sponsor cannot be in all of those with you, and it is not fair to ask them to be.

That is the gap. Not a gap in wisdom, a gap in coverage. An AI you can text is a reasonable thing to put in it, in the same way a meeting list in your pocket or a phone tree is a reasonable thing to put in it. It is a tool for the middle of the night that helps you get to the morning, when you can call a human being.

A test worth applying to anything, including us

Does this tool make it more likely you will talk to a person, or less? If a product is built so that you never need to call anyone, be careful with it. Isolation is the thing you are recovering from. A tool that quietly helps you avoid people is working against you, however good it feels to use.

What an AI sponsor is not

Being clear about this matters more than the marketing does.

It is not a therapist or a doctor. It cannot diagnose you, it cannot treat you, and it has no idea what withdrawal is doing to your body. Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal in particular can be dangerous and sometimes fatal without medical supervision. If you are detoxing, that is a doctor's job, not a chatbot's.

It is not a crisis service. If you are in danger, you need people who can actually reach you. The numbers are at the bottom of this page.

It is not affiliated with AA, NA, or any 12-step fellowship. We are not, and no similar product is. The fellowships do not endorse apps. Anything implying otherwise is misrepresenting itself. AA's own traditions keep it out of endorsements entirely, which is part of why it has lasted.

It is not anonymous in the way a meeting is. A meeting is a room of people bound by a tradition. An app is a company with servers. Read what any recovery product actually says about where your words go and who can read them, ours included, and judge it on what it says rather than on how private it feels to type into.

How do you use one without hiding behind it?

The failure mode is not that the AI gives bad advice. It is that it is easier than a person, and easier wins by default.

A few things that keep it in its place:

  1. Keep a human sponsor. If you do not have one yet, getting one is the actual goal, and the section below is about that.
  2. Notice if your calls are dropping off. If you used to phone your sponsor twice a week and now you type instead, that is not convenience, that is avoidance wearing convenience as a coat.
  3. Tell your sponsor you are using it. Recovery runs on not having a private compartment. A tool you would rather not mention is worth mentioning.
  4. Use it to prepare, not to substitute. Draft the inventory at 2am, bring it to a person at 2pm.
  5. Keep going to meetings. Nothing on a screen replaces a room of people who know what you are talking about.

How do you find a human sponsor, including online?

Since this is the part that actually matters, here it is properly.

Go to meetings and stay afterwards. This is still how most sponsorship starts. The conversation after the meeting is where it happens, not during it.

Listen for someone whose recovery you want. The usual advice is to ask someone who has what you want and has done the steps themselves. Not the most impressive person in the room, the one who sounds like they are telling the truth.

Ask directly. It is a bigger moment for you than for them. Most people who sponsor have been asked before and remember doing the asking.

If you cannot get to a room, go online. AA's online intergroup lists meetings running at every hour in most time zones. Join a few minutes early and say in the chat that you are looking for a sponsor. Many online groups keep a list of members willing to sponsor remotely, and some have a form for exactly this. Sponsorship by phone and video is normal now and is not a lesser version of it.

Take a temporary sponsor in the meantime. Plenty of groups will pair you with someone for a few weeks, no commitment either way. It is a much smaller ask and it gets you a phone number tonight.

If you are in crisis right now

Please contact a person, not an app. In the US, call or text 988 for the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, or text HOME to 741741 for the Crisis Text Line. Both are free, confidential, and available 24 hours a day. Outside the US, your local emergency number is the right call.

Where we stand

We build an AI sponsor, so treat this next part with the scepticism it deserves.

We built it because the gap in the middle of the night is real and most people in recovery have sat in it. We did not build it to replace anybody, and if it ever starts pulling people away from meetings and sponsors, it is doing the opposite of the job. It is for the hours in between. That is the whole claim, and it is deliberately a small one.
